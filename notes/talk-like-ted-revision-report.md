# 1. Book audit summary for Talk Like TED by Carmine Gallo

The existing Talk Like TED package was not publishable. The book itself has a clear and distinctive communication framework, but the current package reduced that framework to generic template prose that repeated the same patterns across nearly every chapter.

The summaries were especially weak. Paragraph two was essentially the same in every chapter, which erased the actual logic of the book and made nine different lessons sound like one recycled lesson about vague decision making.

The bullet sets were repetitive, the scenarios reused the same flat shell with only a few nouns changed, and the quizzes relied on formula prompts instead of authentic applied judgment. The result did not feel like a guided lesson. It felt like placeholder content wearing book specific titles.

The motivation layer was also mismatched to the book. Generic tone tails about conflict and pressure did not fit a communication title built around connection, memorability, and audience experience. This revision therefore replaces the book package chapter by chapter and adds book specific motivation handling in the reader.

# 2. Main content problems found

1. Summaries repeated generic structure instead of explaining the real lesson of each chapter.
2. Bullet points often sounded like slogans and reused the same detail language across the whole book.
3. Scenarios were too similar to one another and rarely showed believable transfer of the actual speaking principle.
4. Quiz prompts were formulaic and tested recognition of a template instead of understanding of the lesson.
5. Depth variation was weak because the content did not create a meaningful jump from Simple to Standard to Deeper.
6. Motivation personalization felt fake because the tone logic was generic and not tailored to a speaking and storytelling book.

# 3. Personalization strategy for Talk Like TED by Carmine Gallo

Depth remains the authored content axis. Simple now uses seven strong bullets for fast understanding, Standard uses ten complete bullets as the premium default, and Deeper uses fifteen bullets that add real nuance about audience psychology, preparation, tradeoffs, and transfer.

Motivation remains a guidance axis rather than nine duplicated book files. The package keeps the core lesson stable while the reader applies Talk Like TED specific coaching language to summary framing, scenario guidance, recap language, and quiz explanations.

Gentle for this book emphasizes calm human connection, Direct emphasizes clarity and disciplined editing, and Competitive emphasizes the edge that comes from being memorable in rooms full of forgettable talks.

This approach keeps the schema lean, preserves fidelity, and still makes the user experience across Simple, Standard, Deeper and Gentle, Direct, Competitive feel materially different.

# 4. Any minimal schema adjustments needed

No package schema change was required. The revision stays inside the current JSON shape. Depth is authored in the package, and motivation is handled in the reader through Talk Like TED specific guidance instead of nine duplicated package variants.

### Scenarios

1. Science fair opener (SCHOOL)
Scenario: A student built a water quality project after her younger brother got sick from a bad camping trip, but her presentation opens with definitions and lab terms. The class understands the topic, but nobody feels why the project matters.
What to do: Open with the moment that made the issue personal to you. Then connect that moment to the evidence you want the class to understand.
Why it matters: The hidden problem is not lack of information. It is lack of visible stake. Conviction turns the facts from a school report into a message worth caring about.

2. Debate that feels flat (SCHOOL)
Scenario: A student is arguing for later school start times, but he sounds detached because he is trying to imitate formal debate language. His points are reasonable, yet the room does not feel any urgency.
What to do: Say plainly why the issue matters to students instead of hiding behind generic phrasing. Use one concrete experience that shows you mean what you are saying.
Why it matters: People trust seriousness more when they hear a real stake. The point is not to become dramatic. It is to stop speaking as if the issue belongs to no one.

3. Product pitch with no spark (WORK)
Scenario: A product manager is pitching an accessibility feature, but the deck begins with roadmap labels and stakeholder jargon. She actually cares deeply about the users who keep dropping out, yet that concern never reaches the room.
What to do: Lead with the user problem that genuinely bothers you. Let that concern shape the talk before you move into roadmap detail.
Why it matters: Conviction gives technical content purpose. When the room sees what you are trying to protect, the feature stops sounding like a random request.

4. Status update nobody remembers (WORK)
Scenario: A team lead gives a quarterly update on customer churn. He includes every metric he collected, but the talk sounds like inventory instead of leadership.
What to do: Frame the update around the outcome you believe the team must protect. Cut any metric that does not strengthen that central concern.
Why it matters: Caring about the message should narrow the talk, not inflate it. Visible conviction helps people see what deserves their attention first.

5. Wedding toast by committee (PERSONAL)
Scenario: A best friend writes a wedding toast with safe facts and polite compliments because she is scared of sounding too emotional. The result is accurate but forgettable.
What to do: Choose the one truth about the couple you most want everyone to feel. Build the toast around that truth instead of trying to cover everything.
Why it matters: A toast becomes memorable when the speaker is willing to stand inside a real feeling. That focus gives the audience something genuine to carry.

6. Community meeting request (PERSONAL)
Scenario: A neighbor wants people to volunteer for a park cleanup, but he sounds procedural and distant. Residents hear the logistics and ignore the cause.
What to do: Tell the room why the park matters to you personally. Use that reason to invite people into the effort before you discuss the schedule.
Why it matters: People respond to a living reason more than to a list of tasks. Genuine conviction helps ordinary requests sound worth joining.

### Scenarios

1. History comes alive (SCHOOL)
Scenario: A student is presenting on the civil rights movement with dates, laws, and quotes, but the class is drifting. The material is important, yet it feels distant because nobody can picture a human life inside it.
What to do: Replace the overview opening with one short story about a person living through the event. Use the broader facts after the class understands what was at stake for that person.
Why it matters: The hidden issue is not lack of content. It is lack of human sequence. A story turns a pile of facts into a pattern the class can feel and remember.

2. Club recruitment pitch (SCHOOL)
Scenario: A student leader is recruiting volunteers for a tutoring club and leads with duties, hours, and scheduling needs. The room nods politely but does not commit.
What to do: Start with a brief story about one student who benefited from the club. Then show how that story connects to the roles you need filled.
Why it matters: A human example helps people understand what the work actually means. The request becomes concrete instead of administrative.

3. Sales deck with no human face (WORK)
Scenario: A sales team presents a strong analytics deck, but the buyer hears only percentages and feature tables. The actual customer pain never feels real.
What to do: Tell one client story that shows the problem before you show the data. Use the metrics after the room can picture the human cost of doing nothing.
Why it matters: The story gives the numbers a job. Instead of floating as proof by themselves, they now deepen a situation the buyer already understands.

4. Safety training nobody feels (WORK)
Scenario: A manager is leading a safety refresher after a near miss, but the talk opens with policy language. Employees know the rules already and tune out.
What to do: Open with the recent incident in a respectful, specific narrative. Then connect the policy reminders to the decisions people faced inside that moment.
Why it matters: Stories sharpen attention because they show consequence in sequence. The training stops feeling like repeated policy and starts feeling like lived judgment.

5. Family donation request (PERSONAL)
Scenario: Someone wants relatives to support a local food bank and starts with annual totals and budget gaps. The numbers are real, but the appeal feels abstract.
What to do: Tell a short real story that shows the need instead of leading with totals. Use the numbers after the family can picture who the help is for.
Why it matters: People often act more readily once the issue has a human face. The story gives meaning to the numbers instead of asking the numbers to do everything.

6. Roommate reset (PERSONAL)
Scenario: A roommate wants to fix an uneven chore split and plans to lecture with a list of complaints. The facts are solid, but the talk may sound like prosecution.
What to do: Use one recent scene that captures the problem everyone already recognizes. Then move from that scene to a simpler shared plan.
Why it matters: A concrete scene lowers abstraction and helps the other person see the issue instead of defending against a long argument.

1. Too many slides for class (SCHOOL)

4. Training with crowded screens (WORK)

What to do: Simplify the slides so they support listening instead of competing with it. Provide detailed reference material separately if people need it after the session.
Why it matters: The problem is split attention. Visual support should reduce mental load, not double it.

5. Wedding toast that keeps growing (PERSONAL)
Scenario: Someone keeps adding stories to a wedding toast because every memory feels important. By the end, the emotion has diluted into repetition.
What to do: Keep one through line and cut stories that do not deepen it. Trust that one well chosen memory can honor the moment better than six scattered ones.
Why it matters: Length often weakens warmth by smearing it across too many moments. Compression helps the meaning stay strong.

6. House rules lecture (PERSONAL)
Scenario: A parent wants to explain new house rules and turns the conversation into a long speech. Halfway through, nobody is really listening anymore.
What to do: Reduce the talk to the few rules that actually matter now. Deliver them in short sections so each point has room to register.
Why it matters: Attention is not won by force. Clear prioritization makes the message more likely to stick and be followed.

### Scenarios

1. Quiet student copying a captain (SCHOOL)
Scenario: A student preparing for a competition tries to imitate the loudest and most theatrical speaker on the team. The copied style makes him tense and less clear than usual.
What to do: Build the talk around calm clarity and the strengths you can deliver honestly. Borrow the structure you admire without copying the personality wrapped around it.
Why it matters: The problem is not low energy. It is poor fit. A style that matches the speaker is easier to trust and easier to sustain under pressure.

2. Artist forcing formal delivery (SCHOOL)
Scenario: A creative student gives strong explanations in conversation but turns stiff and over formal during presentations because she thinks that is what serious speakers sound like.
What to do: Use the visual and expressive strengths you naturally bring to explanation. Keep the rigor of the content while letting the delivery sound more like your real thinking voice.
Why it matters: Authenticity is not the enemy of seriousness. When style and person align, the message often becomes clearer and more memorable.

3. Technical founder playing entertainer (WORK)
Scenario: A founder keeps forcing big stage energy into investor updates because he thinks that is what strong presenters do. The performance distracts from the sharp product insight he actually brings.
What to do: Lean into the clarity, precision, and demonstrations that are naturally your strength. Use energy to support that strength instead of trying to replace it with borrowed showmanship.
Why it matters: Memorability does not require imitation. The strongest lane is the one where your real advantage becomes easiest for the room to notice.

4. Manager mimicking a keynote style (WORK)
Scenario: A manager who is excellent one on one starts copying a famous keynote speaker's gestures and catchphrases for town halls. Employees notice the performance before they notice the message.
What to do: Adapt the strengths people already trust in you to the larger room. Borrow techniques that fit your voice instead of importing someone else's persona.
Why it matters: A borrowed shell often hides the quality the audience actually values. Fit makes leadership communication easier to believe.

5. Speech borrowed from the internet (PERSONAL)
Scenario: Someone writing a wedding speech keeps copying the rhythm of viral toasts online and ends up sounding unlike herself. The lines are polished, but the emotion feels second hand.
What to do: Use your own tone and memories even if the language is simpler. Keep only the techniques that help your real voice come through more clearly.
Why it matters: People remember sincerity more than borrowed brilliance. The speech should sound like the person giving it, not like an algorithm of good speeches.

6. Community leader using influencer voice (PERSONAL)
Scenario: A neighborhood organizer starts speaking in a highly branded social media tone because it seems more charismatic. Longtime volunteers feel the shift immediately and pull back.
What to do: Return to the grounded style that matches how people already trust you. Add stronger structure and clearer calls to action without adopting a false persona.
Why it matters: Authenticity is strategic because it protects trust. A style that feels borrowed can cost credibility even if it sounds energetic.
